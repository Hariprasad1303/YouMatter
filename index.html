Frequently asked questions

Mental health encompasses your emotional, psychological, and social well-being. It affects every aspect of life, including how you think, feel, act, make decisions, and relate to others. Good mental health means being able to cope with the normal stresses of life, be productive in work and activities, contribute to your community, and realize your abilities. Just like physical health, mental health is important at every stage of life, from childhood to adulthood.

Mental health problems can be caused by a combination of factors. These include:

  • Biological factors such as genetics, brain chemistry, or physical illness
  • Psychological factors including trauma, abuse, or significant life changes
  • Environmental influenceslike chronic stress, financial worries, or unstable living conditions

Experiencing symptoms such as persistent sadness, hopelessness, or irritability; withdrawing from friends, family, or social activities; noticeable changes in sleep or eating habits; difficulty concentrating or making decisions; losing interest in activities you once enjoyed; feeling constantly overwhelmed by worries or fears; having unexplained physical issues like headaches or stomachaches; or having thoughts of self-harm are strong indicators that your mental health may be struggling. If these feelings or changes persist for weeks or begin to interfere with your daily responsibilities, relationships, or overall quality of life, it’s important to seek support from a mental health professional. Early help can make a significant difference in recovery and well-being.

Yes, most mental health problems are treatable, and with the right approach, many people can recover fully or manage their symptoms effectively to lead fulfilling lives. Treatment may involve therapy, such as cognitive-behavioral or talk therapy, which helps address underlying thoughts and behaviors; medication prescribed by a doctor to help manage symptoms; participation in support groups and community resources for shared understanding and encouragement; and self-care strategies like regular exercise, mindfulness practices, and healthy daily routines. Early intervention plays a key role in improving outcomes, making it important to seek help as soon as concerns arise.

If you ever feel overwhelmed or concerned about your thoughts or emotions, it’s important to take action early. Start by opening up to someone you trust—whether it’s a friend, family member, or mentor—so you don’t have to carry the burden alone. Consider reaching out to a mental health professional who can provide proper assessment and guidance tailored to your needs. Don’t wait for issues to escalate before seeking help, as early intervention can make a significant difference in recovery. Most importantly, remember that you are not alone—there are people, services, and resources available to support you through difficult times.

Supporting a loved one with mental health challenges starts with listening without judgment and offering genuine emotional support so they feel heard and understood. Encourage them to seek professional help or connect with support groups, and check in with them regularly while showing patience throughout their journey. Offering help with everyday tasks can ease their stress, and taking the time to learn more about their condition will allow you to better understand their experiences. Most importantly, respect their privacy and decisions, but always remind them that you care and are there whenever they need you.

Most psychiatric medications, when prescribed by a healthcare professional and used as directed, are safe. Some myths suggest that all mental health medicines are addictive or harmful, but this is not true. Certain medications may have side effects or require close monitoring. Always consult your doctor before starting, stopping, or changing medication. Never stop taking prescribed medication suddenly without guidance, as this can be dangerous.

Mental health refers to cognitive processes such as thinking, memory, perception, and decision-making, while emotional health focuses on understanding, expressing, and coping with feelings. The two are deeply interconnected, meaning challenges in one area can significantly impact the other—for example, unresolved emotional stress can contribute to mental health difficulties, and mental health struggles can make it harder to manage and process emotions effectively. Together, they play a vital role in overall well-being.

Anxiety disorders and depression are the most common mental health conditions worldwide, with anxiety involving excessive fear, worry, or nervousness that disrupts daily life, and depression characterized by persistent sadness, loss of interest or pleasure, and a range of emotional and physical difficulties. Both can significantly impact a person’s ability to function, but they are also highly treatable, especially when help is sought early through therapy, medication, lifestyle changes, and support systems.
